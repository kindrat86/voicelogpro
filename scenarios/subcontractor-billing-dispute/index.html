Subcontractor Billing Dispute: How Daily Reports Settle Scope Claims

Real scenario: Electrical subcontractor overbills by $28K. GC uses VoiceLogPro daily reports to prove actual scope. Dispute resolved in 3 days.

An electrical subcontractor submitted a $67,000 billing that included $28,000 of work the GC had no record of. Here's how detailed daily reports resolved the dispute in 72 hours.

The Scenario

Month 6 of a $12M office build. The electrical subcontractor's monthly billing included 340 hours of 'additional rough-in' that the GC's superintendent did not recall authorizing. The GC pushed back; the sub threatened to walk off.

The Evidence

The GC had 180 days of VoiceLogPro daily reports. Each report documented:

  • Which subcontractors were on-site (by company and crew member name)
  • Hours worked by each subcontractor crew
  • Specific work areas accessed (floor, room, zone)
  • Work performed by trade (e.g., 'electricians: rough-in conference room 4A, 6 hours')
  • Change orders authorized that day (with owner approval reference)

The Resolution

The GC compiled 23 daily reports showing the electrical crew's actual hours (212 hours, not 340) and work areas. The reports were timestamped and signed. The subcontractor reviewed the documentation and reduced the billing to $42,000. Dispute resolved without legal action.

Key lesson: Without daily reports, the GC would have had to pay $67,000 or face a work stoppage. With VoiceLogPro reports, the actual scope was documented and defensible.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can daily reports override a subcontractor's billing?

Daily reports are the most powerful contemporaneous evidence of actual work performed. If your daily reports document 212 hours and the sub bills 340, the daily reports create a strong rebuttable presumption. Most disputes settle before arbitration when confronted with consistent daily documentation.

What if I forgot to document a change order?

Document it as soon as you realize the omission. A late daily report entry noting 'change order X was verbally authorized on [date] by [person]' is weaker than a contemporaneous entry but far better than nothing. Always get change orders in writing.

How detailed should subcontractor documentation be in daily reports?

Include: company name, crew member names, hours (arrival/departure), work area, task description, and any issues. The level of detail determines how useful the report will be in a dispute.
