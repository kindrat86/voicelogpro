Mechanic's Lien Enforcement: How Daily Reports Prove Your Claim

Real scenario: Plumber enforces a $42K mechanic's lien using VoiceLogPro daily reports. Learn the 7 documentation points that made the claim enforceable.

A plumbing subcontractor was owed $42,000 on a commercial project when the GC went bankrupt. The owner claimed the plumber's work was incomplete. Here's how daily reports proved the claim.

The Scenario

The plumber had completed rough-in and first-fix plumbing for 12 bathroom groups in a hotel renovation. The GC filed bankruptcy. The hotel owner refused payment, claiming the work was incomplete and defective. The plumber filed a mechanic's lien.

The Evidence

The plumber's VoiceLogPro daily reports documented:

  • Exact dates on-site (14 days over 6 weeks, matching the claim period)
  • Crew members present (2 licensed plumbers by name and license number)
  • Specific work completed by day and location (e.g., 'rough-in bathroom groups 1-4, second floor, 8 hours')
  • Materials furnished with quantities (120 feet of 3-inch PVC, 24 fixture rings, 12 shut-off valve assemblies)
  • Inspection results (passed rough-in inspection on [date], inspector name and number)
  • Photos of completed work with timestamps
  • Last day on-site (establishing the lien filing deadline clock)

The Outcome

Result: The court enforced the full $42,000 lien plus legal costs. The owner's claim of 'incomplete work' was rebutted by the inspection pass records in the daily reports. The bankruptcy trustee paid the lien from project funds.

The 7 Documentation Points That Matter

  1. Dates on-site — establishes the claim period and filing deadline
  2. Crew identity — proves licensed labor was furnished
  3. Work scope by day — proves what was completed vs claimed
  4. Materials with quantities — supports the dollar amount
  5. Inspection records — proves work met code
  6. Photos with timestamps — visual proof of completion
  7. Last furnishing date — starts the lien filing clock

Frequently Asked Questions

How long do I have to file a mechanic's lien after my last day on-site?

It varies by state: 60 days (Louisiana, Nevada) to 240 days (Wisconsin). Most states are 90-180 days. The clock starts from your last day furnishing labor or materials, which your daily report documents.

Do I need daily reports to file a mechanic's lien?

Legally no, but practically yes. Without daily reports, you must reconstruct your scope and dates from memory, receipts, and delivery tickets. Courts give far more weight to contemporaneous daily reports. For lien enforcement, they are the difference between winning and losing.

What if my daily reports are incomplete?

Incomplete reports are still better than no reports. Supplement with any available evidence: delivery receipts, inspection records, correspondence, and witness testimony. But going forward, use VoiceLogPro to ensure every day is documented.
