Weather Delay Dispute: How Daily Reports Win Time Extensions

Real scenario: Contractor uses VoiceLogPro daily reports to win a 12-day time extension on a $4M project. Learn the documentation that made it work.

A general contractor on a $4M commercial build in Chicago faced a 14-day weather delay in January. The owner denied the time extension claim. Here's how contemporaneous daily reports won the dispute.

The Scenario

January 2026. Chicago. Temperatures dropped below 10°F for 9 consecutive days, making exterior masonry work impossible. The GC requested a 14-day time extension. The owner countered that only 5 days were truly unworkable.

The Evidence

The GC had 47 days of VoiceLogPro daily reports covering the disputed period. Each report included:

  • Daily temperature readings (high/low) recorded at 7 AM and 2 PM
  • Wind chill calculations with specific values
  • Ground condition notes (frozen, snow accumulation, ice)
  • Crew attempts to work (arrived, assessed conditions, stood down)
  • Photos of site conditions with timestamps
  • Materials that could not be installed (temperature-dependent products)

The Outcome

Result: The arbitrator granted 12 of the 14 requested days. The 2-day reduction was for a day where the GC could not prove crew mobilization and a day with borderline temperatures.

Why It Worked

The daily reports were: (1) contemporaneous (made the same day), (2) specific (exact temperatures, not 'cold'), (3) consistent (same format every day), and (4) included mitigation attempts (showing the GC tried to work). The owner's counter-argument relied on NOAA averages, which the arbitrator dismissed as 'industry averages, not site-specific evidence.'

Frequently Asked Questions

What temperature makes exterior construction work impossible?

It depends on the trade. Masonry work generally cannot proceed below 20°F (per ACI 530). Concrete placement requires 40°F and rising. Steel erection is limited above 25°F wind chill. Document the specific trade requirements in your daily report.

How do I document weather delays in my daily report?

Record: actual site temperature (not the forecast), wind speed, precipitation, ground conditions, what work was planned, why it could not proceed, and what mitigation was attempted. Specific numbers win disputes; 'too cold' loses.

Do I need a weather service report to support my claim?

Contemporaneous daily reports with your own site readings are stronger evidence than NOAA data, because they reflect actual site conditions. Supplement with NOAA data to show the weather event was regional, not just your site.
