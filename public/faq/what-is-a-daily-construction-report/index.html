What is a daily construction report?

TL;DR: A daily construction report is a contemporaneous written record of work performed, crew hours, materials, weather, equipment, and any delays or safety issues on a construction project. It serves as legal evidence for payment disputes, mechanics lien claims, and OSHA compliance. A proper daily report is created at or near the time of the work — this contemporaneous creation is what makes it admissible as a business record under Federal Rule of Evidence 803(6) and state equivalents.

What a Complete Daily Report Includes

A thorough daily construction report documents every aspect of the day's work. Courts and arbitrators look for consistent, complete entries that follow the same format every day — selective record-keeping undermines credibility.

  • Project name and date — Basic identifiers that tie the report to a specific project and day
  • Weather conditions — Temperature, precipitation, wind speed, and general conditions. This converts subjective claims ("it was too wet to pour") into objective evidence ("0.6 inches of rain, high of 52°F")
  • Crew members and hours — Names of workers on site, their trades, and hours worked. Critical for proving labor costs in change order and delay claims
  • Work performed summary — What was accomplished, where, and to what specification. Include quantities where possible (linear feet of conduit, squares of roofing, etc.)
  • Materials delivered and used — What arrived, what was consumed, and any shortages or quality issues. This verifies material costs for lien amounts and change orders
  • Equipment on site — Heavy equipment, tools, and machinery present and in use. Document idle time for delay claims
  • Delays and issues — Any interruptions, trade stacking, material shortages, design conflicts, or force majeure events. This is the most legally significant section — contemporaneous delay documentation is the strongest evidence in a delay claim
  • Safety observations and incidents — Near-misses, hazards identified and corrected, toolbox talks conducted, and any OSHA-recordable events
  • Photos with metadata — Time-stamped, geotagged photos showing work progress, site conditions, and any issues. Photos without metadata can be challenged as not from the relevant date or location
  • Inspections completed — Which inspections passed or failed, who performed them, and any corrective actions required

Who Uses Daily Construction Reports?

Daily reports serve different purposes for different stakeholders:

  • Subcontractors (electricians, plumbers, HVAC, roofers) — The primary users. Daily reports protect lien rights by proving work dates, documenting delays, and showing compliance with specifications. Without daily reports, a subcontractor's only evidence in a payment dispute is their word against the GC's.
  • General contractors — GCs use daily reports to track subcontractor progress, document site conditions for owners, and create an audit trail for schedule and budget performance.
  • Project owners — Owners review daily reports to verify progress against schedules before approving draw requests and change orders.
  • Legal and insurance teams — Attorneys and adjusters rely on daily reports as the most objective record of what happened on a site and when.
