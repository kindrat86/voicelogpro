What Makes a Daily Log App Court-Admissible?

Not all daily log apps produce records that hold up in court. Under the business-records exception to hearsay (Federal Rule of Evidence 803(6) and state equivalents), a daily log is admissible when it has five characteristics:

  1. Contemporaneous creation — The log must be created at or near the time of the work. Apps that allow backdating or editing timestamps undermine admissibility. VoiceLogPro locks the timestamp at capture; the PDF records the exact moment the user spoke the report.
  2. Timestamp verification — Each entry must carry an objective, unalterable date and time. VoiceLogPro embeds capture timestamps in the PDF metadata and the filename itself.
  3. Objective corroboration — Independent data converts subjective claims into verifiable evidence. Weather data, geotagged photos, and GPS coordinates turn "it was raining" into "0.8 inches of rain recorded at 7:30 AM".
  4. Photo evidence with metadata — Photos with embedded timestamp and geolocation prove what a site looked like, where, and when. Photos without metadata can be challenged as "taken some other day."
  5. Consistent daily practice — A daily habit reads as a routine business record. Gaps and one-off entries invite arguments that documentation was created after the dispute arose, not as a matter of regular practice.

Can I use a daily log app for OSHA compliance?

Yes. OSHA requires employers to maintain records of work-related injuries and illnesses (OSHA Form 300), but daily logs serve as the underlying documentation. A well-maintained daily log with safety observations, near-miss reports, and incident documentation is the best evidence of good-faith compliance in an OSHA inspection.
