What is Speech-To-Text?

Speech-to-text — also called automatic speech recognition, or ASR — is the technology that converts spoken audio into written words, live or from a recording. It is the engine behind voice assistants, dictation features, and transcription apps, including tools that turn spoken field notes into construction daily reports.

Speech-To-Text Definition

A speech-to-text system takes an audio signal and produces text, using models trained on large volumes of recorded speech. Modern AI systems handle natural speaking pace, punctuation, and a wide range of accents without per-user training, which is what made phone-based voice documentation practical for field work.

Why Speech-To-Text Matters

For field inspectors, speech-to-text impacts:

  • Operational efficiency
  • Decision-making quality
  • Bottom-line results
  • Risk management

Frequently Asked Questions

What does speech-to-text mean?

Speech-to-text means software that converts spoken language into written text: you speak into a microphone and the system outputs the words you said. It is the general-purpose technology underneath dictation, captions, and transcription products, and the reason a spoken site note can become a written record without a keyboard.

Why is speech-to-text important?

Because the alternative — typing after a long shift — is where field documentation usually dies. Speech-to-text removes the keyboard from the workflow, so notes get captured because capturing them costs almost nothing. More consistent capture means fewer documentation gaps, and gaps are what hurt when a payment dispute surfaces.

How is speech-to-text different from transcription?

Speech-to-text is the technology; transcription is the task. Transcription can be done by a human typist or by a speech-to-text system. When people say AI transcription, they mean transcription performed by speech-to-text software — automated, near-instant, and inexpensive, with accuracy that depends mostly on audio quality.
