What is Transcription?

Transcription is the process of converting spoken audio into written text. On a job site it is what turns a foreman's spoken end-of-day notes into words on a page — the raw material for a daily report, a compliance record, or evidence in a payment dispute when one surfaces later.

Transcription Definition

In voice-first documentation, transcription means converting a recorded voice note into text, either automatically through AI speech recognition or by a human typist. Automated transcription returns text in seconds at minimal cost, while human transcription trades speed for accuracy on difficult audio like windy job-site recordings.

Why Transcription Matters

For field inspectors, transcription impacts:

  • Operational efficiency
  • Decision-making quality
  • Bottom-line results
  • Risk management

Frequently Asked Questions

What does transcription mean?

Transcription means converting speech into written text. A recording of you describing the day's work becomes a text record of the same words. In construction documentation this matters because text can be searched, structured into daily report fields, and attached to disputes, while raw audio is hard to reference.

Why is transcription important?

Because spoken notes are fast to capture but hard to use later. Transcription makes voice records searchable and quotable: you can find the day a delay was first mentioned, copy the exact wording into a notice letter, and hand a written record to a GC, adjuster, or attorney.

How accurate is AI transcription?

Accuracy depends on audio quality: background noise, wind, accents, and technical vocabulary all reduce it. Clean speech recorded close to the phone transcribes well; a demo saw next to a generator does not. The practical fix is reviewing the transcript the same day, while you still remember what you said.
