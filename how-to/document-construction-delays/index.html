Step-by-Step Guide

1. Identify the delay event immediately

Document the cause as soon as it occurs. Weather, owner-directed changes, unforeseen site conditions, or subcontractor delays must be logged the same day.

2. Record the impact on schedule

Note which activities are affected, the duration of the delay, and any critical path impacts. Reference the project schedule activity IDs.

3. Notify the owner in writing

Most contracts require written notice within 3-14 days of the delay event. Include: cause, anticipated impact, and whether you are requesting a time extension.

4. Quantify the cost impact

Track extended general conditions, equipment rentals, labor inefficiency, and material escalation. Daily reports with crew and equipment logs are the primary evidence.

5. Compile the delay claim

Combine daily reports, schedule analysis, correspondence, and cost data into a structured claim. The more contemporaneous documentation you have, the stronger the claim.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is contemporaneous documentation?

Records made at the time an event occurs, not reconstructed afterward. Courts and arbitrators give far more weight to contemporaneous records than to after-the-fact summaries. Daily reports are the most important contemporaneous record.

How long after a delay must I notify the owner?

Most standard contracts (AIA, FIDIC) require notice within 14-21 days. Some require as little as 3 days. Check your contract's notice provisions carefully.

Do I need daily reports for a delay claim?

Not legally required, but practically essential. Without daily reports, you cannot prove the delay's duration, impact, or that mitigation efforts were attempted. Insurance companies and arbitrators expect them.
