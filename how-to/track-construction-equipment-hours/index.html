Step-by-Step Guide

1. Record equipment on-site in daily reports

Log each piece of equipment (make, model, serial number), hours operated, and the work it performed. This supports both billing and mobilization claims.

2. Track operator assignments

Note which operator ran which equipment and for how long. Operator hours and equipment hours may differ and both matter for cost recovery.

3. Monitor fuel consumption

Record fuel type and volume per equipment per day. Fuel costs are reimbursable on many contracts but require documentation.

4. Schedule maintenance based on hours

Use hour meter readings to schedule preventive maintenance. Track oil changes, filter replacements, and inspections against manufacturer intervals.

5. Reconcile with billing system

Match daily report equipment hours to your billing system weekly. Discrepancies between reported and billed hours are the #1 source of billing disputes.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why track equipment hours in daily reports?

Equipment hours logged in daily reports are contemporaneous records that support billing claims, mechanic's liens, and delay costs. They are more defensible than spreadsheet reconstructions.

How accurate do equipment hour logs need to be?

Round to the nearest 0.5 hour. Courts and arbitrators accept reasonable approximations, but logs that are too precise (to the minute) can appear fabricated.

What equipment needs to be tracked?

Any equipment with an hourly billing rate: cranes, excavators, loaders, forklifts, boom lifts, generators, and compressors. Small hand tools typically do not require hourly tracking.
