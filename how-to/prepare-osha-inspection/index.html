Step-by-Step Guide

1. Maintain daily safety documentation

Document safety briefings, PPE compliance, hazard corrections, and incident reports daily. OSHA inspectors will request these records immediately.

2. Keep OSHA 300 logs current

Record all recordable injuries and illnesses within 7 days. The OSHA 300, 300A, and 301 forms must be available for the current year and the previous 5 years.

3. Conduct regular self-audits

Walk the site weekly using OSHA's top 10 violation checklist: fall protection, scaffolding, ladders, respiratory protection, lockout/tagout, PPE, electrical, powered industrial trucks, masonry construction, and eye/face protection.

4. Designate a site representative for inspections

The competent person on-site should know where all documentation is located and be prepared to accompany the inspector. This includes daily reports, safety data sheets, and training records.

5. Document abatement immediately

If cited, document corrective actions with photos and dates. OSHA requires abatement verification within 10 days. Daily reports showing ongoing safety compliance support reduction of penalties.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can daily reports help during an OSHA inspection?

Yes. Daily reports that include safety briefings, hazard corrections, and PPE compliance checks demonstrate a proactive safety culture. Inspectors review these to assess whether violations are isolated or systemic.

What are the most common OSHA citations in construction?

Fall protection (7,500+ citations annually), scaffolding (3,000+), and ladders (2,500+). Fall protection has been the #1 citation for 13 consecutive years.

How much do OSHA citations cost?

Serious citations start at $1,036 each (2024 penalty). Willful or repeated violations can reach $156,632 each. Proper daily safety documentation can reduce penalties by demonstrating good faith.
