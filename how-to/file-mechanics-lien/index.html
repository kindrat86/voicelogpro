Step-by-Step Guide

1. Verify your state's preliminary notice requirement

Most states require a preliminary notice within 15-30 days of first furnishing labor or materials. California requires a 20-day preliminary notice. Texas requires a monthly notice. Missing this forfeits your lien rights.

2. Confirm the filing deadline for your state

Deadlines range from 60 days (Louisiana) to 240 days (Wisconsin) after last furnishing labor or materials. The clock starts the day you last worked, not project completion.

3. Prepare the lien claim document

Include: property description, owner name, amount owed, description of labor/materials furnished, and your contractor license number (if required). Your daily reports are the evidence backbone.

4. File with the county recorder

File in the county where the property is located. Filing fees range from $15 to $75. Some states require notarization. File before the deadline, not after.

5. Send notice to the property owner

Most states require you to send a copy of the recorded lien to the owner within a specified period (often 5-15 days). Certified mail with return receipt is standard.

6. Enforce the lien through foreclosure

If unpaid, you must file a lawsuit to foreclose the lien within 1-2 years (varies by state). If you miss this deadline, the lien expires.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do daily reports help with a mechanic's lien?

Daily reports document exactly what work you performed, when, and for how long. They prove you furnished labor or materials within the claim period. Without daily reports, proving the scope and timing of your work is extremely difficult.

What happens if I miss the filing deadline?

If you miss the deadline, you lose your lien rights permanently. There is no grace period. File early, not on the last day.

Can I file a lien without a written contract?

In most states, yes. An oral contract is enforceable for mechanic's lien purposes. However, daily reports become even more critical as evidence of the agreement's scope.
