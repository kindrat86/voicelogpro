Step-by-Step Guide

1. Record the date, weather, and site conditions

Start with the basics: date, temperature range, precipitation, and ground conditions. Weather delays are the #1 cause of contract disputes. Courts require specific data, not 'it rained.'

2. List all crew members and subcontractors on-site

Include full names, trades, hours worked, and which company they represent. If a subcontractor dispute arises months later, this roster is your proof of who was present.

3. Document work performed by trade

Write what was actually done, not what was planned. 'Poured 42 cubic yards of concrete for the east wing foundation slab' beats 'concrete work continued.'

4. Note materials delivered and used

Record material type, quantity, supplier, and whether it was installed or stored. This supports both billing and future warranty claims.

5. Log equipment on-site

List heavy equipment (cranes, excavators, forklifts) with hours of operation. Equipment logs support cost recovery and prove mobilization.

6. Record visitors and inspections

Note any inspectors, architects, owners, or visitors with arrival and departure times. Inspection results should be documented in detail.

7. Photograph progress and issues

Take photos of completed work, problem areas, and site conditions. Include timestamps. Visual evidence is critical for dispute resolution.

8. Sign, date, and distribute

The superintendent must sign each report. Distribute to the general contractor, owner, and keep a copy. Unsigned reports carry less weight in court.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long do I need to keep construction daily reports?

Retain daily reports for a minimum of 7 years after project completion. Statute of limitations for construction defect claims varies by state but can extend 10+ years. Store digitally with backups.

Can a daily report be used as evidence in court?

Yes. A properly documented daily report made in the ordinary course of business is admissible as a business record under Federal Rule of Evidence 803(6). It must be contemporaneous (made same day), accurate, and routinely produced.

What is the most common mistake in daily reports?

Vagueness. 'Work continued' or 'no issues' are worthless in a dispute. Every entry should answer: who, what, when, where, and how much.
